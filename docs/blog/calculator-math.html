She Was Paying Someone Two Days a Month to Do Math With a Calculator

March 1, 2026 · 4 min read

Two days a month. That's what Sarah's financial reporting cost her.

Not two days of her time — she'd outsourced it. A subcontractor at $200/day came in, pulled numbers from three different systems, typed them into spreadsheets, ran formulas on a calculator, and copy-pasted charts into a slide deck.

Sixteen hours of work. $3,200 a month. $38,400 a year.

For reports that were late half the time and wrong the other half.

The Part That Should Make You Uncomfortable

Sarah isn't bad at her job. She runs a 22-person company. She's sharp. She negotiates contracts, closes deals, manages a team.

But she'd been paying for this reporting setup for three years. Never questioned it. Because it felt like what businesses do — you hire someone, they make the reports, the reports go in a folder, you glance at them before the board meeting.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: she was paying $100/hour for someone to do $15/hour work.

Not because the subcontractor was charging too much. Because the work itself — manual data entry, calculator math, copy-paste formatting — is $15/hour work. It just happened to be wrapped in a monthly invoice that felt normal.

And it was invisible. Nobody in Sarah's company thought to ask: "Why does this take two days?"

What Happened on a Tuesday Afternoon

We set it up in four hours.

No new software to buy. No six-month implementation. No consultants with slide decks about "digital transformation."

We connected her three data sources. Built the logic — the same math her subcontractor did by hand, except without the typos. Added the presentation layer so the output looked like something you'd actually want to show a board.

That was it.

Now her data goes in, the analysis runs, and the presentation comes out. Under an hour. Every month. No missed deadlines. No transposed numbers. No $3,200 invoice.

Before: 16 hours, $3,200/month, reports with errors, delivered late.
After: 47 minutes, automated, accurate, ready before she asks.

The Moment It Clicked

Sarah didn't get excited about the technology. She barely asked how it worked.

What she said was: "I want to run my whole business like this."

Because the reporting was just the first thing she could see. Once it was gone — once the two-day ritual turned into a 47-minute background process — she started looking at everything else.

The proposal formatting that took her office manager half a day. The client onboarding checklist that lived in someone's head. The weekly email summary that three people contributed to and nobody read.

All of it — the same shape. Humans doing mechanical work. Slowly. Expensively. With errors.

Why This Exists and Nobody Told You

Because the people who build this technology talk about it in a language you'd never search for.

They call it "agentic AI" and "workflow automation" and "LLM orchestration." They demo it at conferences you don't attend, in Slack channels you've never heard of, using jargon that sounds like it's from a different industry.

Meanwhile, you're paying someone two days a month to do math with a calculator.

Not because you're behind. Because nobody translated it into your language. Nobody said: "That thing you're annoyed about? The slow reports, the manual busywork, the stuff that feels like it should be faster? It can be. Today. In an afternoon."

The Question

Sarah's subcontractor didn't get fired. He started working on things that actually need a human brain — analysis, recommendations, strategy. The kind of work that's worth $200/day.

But the calculator math? That's done.

So here's what I'd ask you:

What's your version of two days a month with a calculator?

Because you have one. Every business owner does. Some process that takes too long, costs too much, and nobody's questioned in years because "that's just how we do it."

It doesn't have to be financial reporting. It could be client intake. Invoicing. Inventory reconciliation. The weekly status update that takes three hours to compile.

Whatever it is — it probably doesn't need to take two days anymore.
